# The Raindrop Method — Framework Reference

Raindrop (San Diego) is the agency behind Grüns, Dr. Squatch, Native, Lume, Manscaped, Laundry Sauce, and P&G's Spruce launch. They partnered with Grüns at Seed stage and built strategy, identity, mascot, and performance creative from scratch; Grüns launched gummies in Aug 2023 and sold to Unilever for ~$1.2B in under three years. This file distills their publicly evidenced approach into an operating framework.

## 1. The "Their Why" inversion

Founder Jacques Spitzer's core reframe: people don't buy the brand's why (Sinek-style purpose). They buy **their own why** — who they are and who they're hoping to become. Engaging with a brand is an identity performance.

Operational test for any asset: *"What is the customer saying about themselves when they buy/wear/share this?"*
- Dr. Squatch: "I'm a natural man, not a chemical-soap guy" — the ad lets men perform masculinity + self-care simultaneously.
- Grüns: "I'm someone who handles nutrition intelligently but refuses the misery of powders and pills."
- If the answer to the test is generic ("I want to be healthy"), dig one level deeper into identity, not benefit.

Downstream rule: value props are always phrased as identity statements or their direct enablers, never as ingredient lists. Ingredients are reasons-to-believe, not reasons-to-buy.

## 2. Brand platform before creative

Raindrop's Grüns sequence: consumer insights → competitive analysis → avatar development → brand platform → THEN creative. The platform is the durable layer; ads are disposable expressions of it.

A complete platform contains: positioning, brand world, ranked value props in customer language, voice attributes with guardrails, and (if needed) name. Creative made without a platform optimizes for a moment; creative made from a platform compounds — "spark a movement, not a moment."

## 3. The brand world ("Enter the Grüns Zone")

The strongest DTC identities are *enterable*: a place, zone, or state of being rather than a slogan.

Why it works:
- Gives every asset (ad, unboxing, email, retail display) a consistent fictional container.
- Converts purchase into entry — belonging mechanics, not transaction mechanics.
- Extends infinitely: new products are new rooms in the same world, not new brands.

Construction checklist:
- Name the world (Zone, Club, Realm, Mode — pick the noun that matches the avatar's aspiration).
- Define its physics: what's true inside the world that isn't true outside (in the Grüns Zone, nutrition is play, not discipline).
- Define the door: the moment/ritual of entry (first gummy, the pouch-rip, the daily 8).
- Every creative territory (Phase 6) must be a scene set inside the world.

## 4. Mascot theory (the Barry rule)

Raindrop developed "Barry" for Grüns to add personality and approachability **without losing the premium feel** of the identity. That tension — playful edge, premium floor — is the whole discipline.

Mascot value:
- Ownable: competitors can copy a palette overnight; they can't copy a character with accumulated meaning.
- Infinitely reusable: ad spokesperson, packaging moment, email voice, retail standee, plush upsell.
- Cheap variation: one character, unlimited scenarios = endless creative testing without new concepting.

Three mascot types (propose across types in Phase 4):
1. **Character** — a being in the brand world (Barry, the Squatch).
2. **Personified product** — the gummy/bottle itself has a face/attitude.
3. **Embedded device** — personality baked into the mark itself. Grüns' founder chose the ü specifically because it reads as a smiley face; the wordmark IS the mascot.

Premium guardrail (always write it down): list the contexts where the mascot must NOT appear (e.g., clinical claims sections, price-objection handling, apology emails) and the tonal floor (never gross-out, never desperate, never mocking the customer).

## 5. Sub-universe extension

When Grüns expanded to kids, Raindrop didn't rebrand — they built a sub-universe inside the same world aimed at a new avatar (health-conscious parents wanting a fun, effective way to get kids eating greens). Pattern:
- Same world physics, new inhabitant.
- New avatar gets its own Phase-1 identity ladder (the parent's why: "I'm the parent who found the trick, not the parent who fights at dinner").
- Visual system: same primitive tokens, new semantic accent layer — which is exactly why Phase 5 mandates the primitive/semantic token split.

## 6. Channel sequencing (the Grüns arc)

Observed sequence, generalizable:
1. **DTC + email first.** Prove the identity converts with paid performance creative and an email machine. This is where the platform gets pressure-tested and iterated cheaply.
2. **Retail second** (Grüns: Sprouts, Target, Walmart national in 2025). Retail-readiness requires: front-of-pack that wins the 1-second shelf read, awareness floor from DTC spend, and velocity story for buyers.
3. **Broadcast + audience expansion third.** TV supported the retail footprint; Spanish-language creative extended reach. Rule: mass media amplifies distribution that already exists; it doesn't create it.

Anti-pattern: buying broadcast/brand awareness before the DTC machine proves the platform converts.

## 7. Performance-first identity decisions

"As a direct-to-consumer startup, performance wasn't optional — it had to drive real, scalable results from day one." Every identity element gets stress-tested against paid media before it's locked:
- Does the name read in a 1-second thumbnail?
- Does the palette pop in-feed against the platform's chrome (white/black UI)?
- Does the mascot create a hook in the first 2 seconds of video?
- Does the brand world give copywriters an endless hook supply?

If an identity element is beautiful but unhookable, it fails.

## 8. Anti-patterns (reject these when they appear)

- **Moodboard-first branding**: aesthetics chosen before the avatar's identity ladder exists.
- **Founder's-why copy**: leading with the founder's mission instead of the customer's identity.
- **Slogan-as-world**: a tagline pretending to be a brand world (test: can you set a scene inside it?).
- **Mascot without guardrails**: playfulness that erodes the premium floor (or premium that strangles the fun).
- **One-off creative**: single ads instead of repeatable territory formats.
- **Template sameness**: adopting every category code; keep the legitimacy codes, break at least one sameness code deliberately.
- **Broadcast too early**: mass media before distribution and DTC proof.
